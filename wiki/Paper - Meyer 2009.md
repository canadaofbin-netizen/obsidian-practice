# A meta-analytic investigation into the moderating effects of situational strength (Meyer et al., 2009)

### 1) Metadata & Summary
* **Title:** A meta-analytic investigation into the moderating effects of situational strength on the conscientiousness–performance relationship
* **Authors:** Rustin D. Meyer, Reeshad S. Dalal, and Silvia Bonaccio
* **Publication:** Journal of Organizational Behavior, 30, 1077–1102 (2009)
* **Summary:** This meta-analysis demonstrates that occupation-level situational strength is a crucial moderator of the relationship between conscientiousness and job performance. By conceptualizing situational strength through two facets—constraints and consequences—the authors found that conscientiousness is a much stronger predictor of performance in "weak" occupations (where employees have high autonomy and fewer severe consequences for errors) than in "strong" occupations (where behavior is highly constrained by external rules or severe consequences).

### 2) Research Gap & Problem
While organizational scientists have long endorsed "interactionism" (the idea that behavior is influenced by both the person and the situation), debates around personality testing for job performance have largely focused on mean effect sizes rather than situational variability. A primary barrier has been the lack of agreement on how to conceptualize and measure the structure of situations. Specifically, there was a gap in meta-analytically testing whether "situational strength" actually moderates the criterion-related validity of a major personality predictor like conscientiousness.

### 3) Hypotheses / RQs
* **Hypothesis 1:** The conscientiousness–performance relationship is stronger in occupations low in constraints than in occupations high in constraints.
* **Hypothesis 2:** The conscientiousness–performance relationship is stronger in occupations low in consequences than in occupations high in consequences.

### 4) Methodology Details
* **Sample Size:** 114 primary studies, yielding 162 independent correlations, encompassing a total of 34,659 participants across 13 U.S. Standard Occupational Classification codes. 
* **Variables:** 
  * *Predictor:* Trait conscientiousness.
  * *Criteria:* Job performance (overall, task, and contextual).
  * *Moderators:* Constraints, Consequences, and a Global Situational Strength composite. Operationalized using quantitative occupational requirement data from the O*NET database.
* **Models:** Modified Weighted Least Squares (WLS) regression equations.

### 5) Detailed Findings
* **Constraints:** Significantly moderated the conscientiousness–overall performance relationship ($\beta$ = -.16, $p < .05$).
* **Consequences:** Significantly moderated the conscientiousness–performance relationship for overall performance ($\beta$ = -.23, $p < .05$) and task performance ($\beta$ = -.21, $p < .05$).
* **Global Situational Strength:** Exhibited a significant moderating effect for overall ($\beta$ = -.26, $p < .01$) and task performance ($\beta$ = -.22, $p < .05$).
* **Effect Sizes:** Uncorrected effects for overall performance ranged from $r = .09$ in "strong" occupations to $r = .23$ in "weak" occupations.

### 6) Implications
* **Theory and Research:** Validates the situational strength framework by operationalizing it via constraints and consequences at the occupational level.
* **Personality Debate:** Re-frames the question from "does personality predict performance?" to "under what conditions does it predict performance?".

### 7) Limitations
* **O\*NET Aggregation:** Represents average conditions for a "typical" setting, failing to capture variability within the same occupation across different organizations or time.
* **Contextual Performance:** Insufficient primary studies measuring contextual performance to yield stable moderator effects.

### 8) Key Quotes
* "The present study meta-analytically demonstrates that occupation-level situational strength... moderates the conscientiousness–performance relationship, such that conscientiousness better predicts performance in characteristically weak occupations than in characteristically strong occupations."
